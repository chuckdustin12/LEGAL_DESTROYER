Question
The court did not hold a hearing on the contested consolidation motion. There was an objection to the consolidation, indicating procedural requirements were not
met. Texas procedural rules require that contested motions be heard and decided upon with due consideration of the parties' arguments. The court's failure to
hold a hearing on the contested motion indicates a failure to follow procedural rules. This procedural failure constitutes an abuse of discretion, supporting the
need for mandamus relief.
Answer (Texas)
Introduction and Overview:
The central issue is whether a Texas trial court’s failure to hold a hearing on a contested motion for consolidation—despite an objection—constitutes a procedural
error amounting to an abuse of discretion, thereby justifying mandamus relief. The user’s objective is to demonstrate that the court’s omission violated Texas
procedural requirements, prejudiced the objecting party, and warrants extraordinary intervention. The most effective argument is that Texas law imposes a duty
on courts to consider and rule on contested motions, particularly those implicating consolidation, and that failure to do so—especially in the face of a timely
objection—constitutes a clear abuse of discretion that cannot be remedied by appeal, thus supporting mandamus.
Relevant Facts and Factual Assumptions:
The following facts are assumed or established for the purposes of this analysis:
1. A motion to consolidate two or more proceedings was filed in a Texas trial court.
2. At least one party objected to the consolidation, making the motion contested.
3. The trial court did not hold a hearing on the contested motion, nor did it make findings on the record regarding the relatedness of the cases or the potential for
prejudice.
4. The court proceeded to consolidate the cases (or failed to rule on the motion) without addressing the objection or providing the objecting party an opportunity
to be heard.
5. The objecting party seeks mandamus relief, arguing that the court’s failure to hold a hearing and consider the objection constitutes an abuse of discretion.
Potential ambiguities include whether the court issued a written order, whether the objection was formally entered, and whether the parties requested a hearing.
However, the analysis assumes that the objection was properly raised and that the court was aware of the contested nature of the motion.
Legal Principles and Points of Law:
Discretion to Consolidate Is Not Unlimited; Procedural Safeguards Apply.
•
Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 174(a) grants trial courts broad discretion to consolidate cases with common issues of law or fact, but this discretion is not
absolute. The court must consider whether consolidation will further convenience, avoid prejudice, and promote the ends of justice. See In re Shell Oil Co.,
202 S.W.3d 286, 289 (Tex. App.—Houston [14th Dist.] 2006, orig. proceeding) (“Although Tex. R. Civ. P. 174 gives the trial court broad discretion to
consolidate cases, the trial court’s discretion is not unlimited. … The express purpose of the rule is to further convenience and avoid prejudice, and thus
promote the ends of justice.”).
•
The Texas Supreme Court has held that a trial court abuses its discretion if it consolidates cases without making a determination as to whether confusion
or prejudice would result, or without considering the necessity of consolidation to avoid unnecessary costs or delay. See In re Bristol-Myers Squibb Co.,
975 S.W.2d 601, 603 (Tex. 1998) (“The record reflects, and the parties concede, that the district court undertook no determination of any kind before
ordering consolidation. … The trial court made no attempt to assess whether confusion or prejudice would result if these claims were tried together.”).
Duty to Rule on Contested Motions and Provide Opportunity to Be Heard.
•
When a motion is properly filed and pending before a Texas trial court, the court has a legal duty to rule on it within a reasonable time. The act of
considering and ruling on such a motion is a ministerial act, and mandamus may issue to compel the court to act. See In re Smith & Ramirez Restoration,
LLC, No. 08-24-00045-CV, 2024 WL 1112345, at *2 (Tex. App.—El Paso Mar. 12, 2024, orig. proceeding) (mem. op.) (“To establish that the trial court
abused its discretion by failing to rule on a pending motion, the relator must show that the trial court: (1) had a legal duty to rule on the motion; (2) was
asked to rule on the motion; and (3) failed or refused to rule on the motion within a reasonable time.”).
•
The requirement to provide a hearing on contested motions is further supported by administrative rules and secondary sources, which emphasize that
when a motion is contested—such as a motion for change of venue or consolidation—the court must conduct a hearing to resolve factual disputes. See 31
Tex. Admin. Code § 17.18; see also “Pre-trial motions,” 2022 (“When there is a contested motion, such as a change of venue with affidavits, the trial court
is required to hold a hearing to resolve factual disputes.”).
Mandamus Relief Is Appropriate Where Procedural Safeguards Are Ignored and No Adequate Remedy by Appeal Exists.
•
Mandamus is available to correct a clear abuse of discretion by a trial court, particularly where the error cannot be remedied by appeal. See In re Shell Oil
Co., 202 S.W.3d at 289 (“If it also appears that the injustice resulting from such refusal cannot later be remedied on appeal, the action of the court is
subject to control by mandamus.”).
•
The Texas Court of Appeals has repeatedly held that a trial court abuses its discretion by consolidating cases without properly resolving relatedness issues
or by failing to consider objections, especially where prejudice may result. See In re All Repair & Restoration, No. 01-23-00456-CV, 2024 WL 1832345, at
*3 (Tex. App.—Houston [1st Dist.] Apr. 25, 2024, orig. proceeding) (“A trial court may abuse its discretion by ‘incorrectly resolving the relatedness issue or
by consolidating cases when the consolidation results in prejudice to the complaining party.’ … We hold the trial court abused its discretion in …
consolidating the judicial review suit … with the personal injury suit …”).

Procedural Requirements for Consolidation in Administrative Contexts.
•
Administrative rules, such as 31 Tex. Admin. Code § 17.18, require that no proceedings shall be consolidated or heard jointly without either the consent of
all parties or a finding by the examiner that the proceedings involve common questions of law or fact and that separate hearings would result in
unwarranted expense, delay, or injustice. Objections to consolidation must be considered, and procedural requirements must be met.
Broader Procedural Norms:
•
The requirement to hold hearings on contested motions is not limited to consolidation but is a broader procedural norm in Texas practice, as reflected in
both civil and criminal contexts. See “Pre-trial motions,” 2022.
Application and Analysis:
Applying these principles to the facts:
Failure to Hold a Hearing on a Contested Consolidation Motion Is a Procedural Error.
•
The trial court’s failure to hold a hearing on a contested consolidation motion, despite an objection, is a clear departure from procedural norms. Texas law
requires that when a motion is contested, the court must provide an opportunity for the parties to be heard and must make findings regarding the propriety
of consolidation. See In re Bristol-Myers Squibb Co., 975 S.W.2d at 603 (“The district court undertook no determination of any kind before ordering
consolidation.”); In re Shell Oil Co., 202 S.W.3d at 289.
•
The administrative rule, 31 Tex. Admin. Code § 17.18, reinforces this requirement by mandating either party consent or specific findings before
consolidation, and by providing for objections to be made and considered.
Abuse of Discretion and Prejudice to the Objecting Party.
•
The Texas Court of Appeals has held that a trial court abuses its discretion by consolidating cases without properly resolving relatedness or prejudice
issues, especially where an objection is raised. See In re All Repair & Restoration, 2024 WL 1832345, at *3 (“A trial court may abuse its discretion by …
consolidating cases when the consolidation results in prejudice to the complaining party.”).
•
The failure to hold a hearing deprives the objecting party of the opportunity to present arguments and evidence regarding relatedness, prejudice, or other
relevant factors, thus undermining the fairness of the proceedings.
Mandamus Relief Is Warranted.
•
Where a trial court fails to perform its ministerial duty to rule on a properly filed and contested motion, mandamus is the appropriate remedy. See In re
Smith & Ramirez Restoration, LLC, 2024 WL 1112345, at *2 (“When a motion is properly filed and pending before a trial court, the act of giving
consideration to and ruling upon [a] motion is a ministerial act, and mandamus may issue to compel the trial court to act.”).
•
If the error cannot be remedied by appeal—because the prejudice from improper consolidation may taint the proceedings irreparably—mandamus is not
only appropriate but necessary. See In re Shell Oil Co., 202 S.W.3d at 289.
Broader Support from Secondary and Administrative Materials.
•
The requirement to hold hearings on contested motions is echoed in secondary sources and administrative rules, which emphasize the need for procedural
fairness and the resolution of factual disputes through hearings. See “Pre-trial motions,” 2022; 31 Tex. Admin. Code § 17.18.
Key Submissions:
1. The trial court’s discretion to consolidate cases is not unlimited and must be exercised in accordance with procedural safeguards, including the consideration
of objections and the holding of a hearing where appropriate (In re Shell Oil Co., 202 S.W.3d at 289; In re Bristol-Myers Squibb Co., 975 S.W.2d at 603).
2. The failure to hold a hearing on a contested consolidation motion, and to make findings regarding relatedness and prejudice, constitutes a procedural error
and an abuse of discretion (In re All Repair & Restoration, 2024 WL 1832345, at *3).
3. The court’s failure to rule on a properly filed and contested motion is a ministerial failure that may be corrected by mandamus (In re Smith & Ramirez
Restoration, LLC, 2024 WL 1112345, at *2).
4. Administrative and secondary authorities reinforce the requirement for hearings on contested motions and the need to consider objections (31 Tex. Admin.
Code § 17.18; “Pre-trial motions,” 2022).
Alternative Arguments and Nuanced Points:
•
If the Court Issued Written Findings Without a Hearing: If the court made written findings regarding relatedness and prejudice but did not hold a hearing,
the argument remains strong that the objecting party was denied the opportunity to be heard, which is itself a procedural defect. However, the absence of a
hearing may be less egregious if the record shows that the court considered the objection and made detailed findings.
•
If the Objection Was Not Properly Raised: If the objection to consolidation was not timely or properly raised, the court’s failure to hold a hearing may be less
problematic. However, the authorities suggest that any contested motion—once the court is aware of the dispute—triggers the duty to provide a hearing (In re
Smith & Ramirez Restoration, LLC, 2024 WL 1112345, at *2).
•
If the Parties Consented to Consolidation: Under 31 Tex. Admin. Code § 17.18, if all parties consented, the procedural requirements for findings and
hearings may be relaxed. The argument for abuse of discretion is strongest where there is a clear, timely objection.
