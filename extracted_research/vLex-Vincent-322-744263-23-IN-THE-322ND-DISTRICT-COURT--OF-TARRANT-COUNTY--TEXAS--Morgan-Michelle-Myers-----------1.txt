Short response
The plaintiff is correct that, under Texas law, a timely request for a de novo
hearing divests an associate judge of jurisdiction over the specified matters,
so any subsequent judicial acts on those matters are taken in the complete
absence of jurisdiction. When an associate judge acts without jurisdiction—
such as compelling execution of orders or denying motions—judicial
immunity does not apply, and such conduct may constitute extortion under
federal law if it involves the wrongful use of official authority to obtain
property or business interests.
Summary
Texas statutes and case law make clear that once a party files a timely
request for a de novo hearing, the associate judge loses all jurisdiction over
the matters specified in the request, and only the referring court may act. If
the associate judge nevertheless compels a party to sign orders or denies
motions on those matters, those acts are outside the scope of judicial
authority and are not protected by judicial immunity.
In this scenario, the associate judge’s actions—compelling the respondent to
sign an order and denying his motion after jurisdiction was divested—were
taken in the complete absence of jurisdiction. Under both Texas and federal
law, such acts may expose the judge to liability, including for extortion under
the Hobbs Act, if the conduct involves the wrongful use of judicial power to
affect property or business interests and interstate commerce.
Background and Relevant Law
Legislative Framework
The Texas Family Code and Government Code provide a detailed structure
for the powers and limitations of associate judges in family law matters. The
most relevant provisions are:
Tex. Fam. Code § 201.015 and § 201.317: These sections allow a
party to request a de novo hearing before the referring court by filing a
written request within three working days after receiving notice of the
associate judge’s report or temporary order. Once such a request is
filed, the associate judge is divested of jurisdiction over the specified
matters, and the referring court assumes exclusive authority.
Tex. Fam. Code § 201.1041 and § 201.2041: These clarify that if a
timely request for a de novo hearing is filed, the associate judge’s
proposed order does not become the order of the referring court by
operation of law; instead, the referring court must act.
Tex. Fam. Code § 201.104: Only the referring court may hear and
render orders on motions for postjudgment relief, including motions to
vacate, correct, or reform a judgment.

Analysis
1. Divestiture of Jurisdiction Upon De Novo Hearing
Request
The Texas Family Code and Government Code are unequivocal: when a party
files a timely request for a de novo hearing, the associate judge is
immediately stripped of jurisdiction over the matters specified in the
request, and only the referring court may act on those matters. This is not a
discretionary or procedural technicality; it is a mandatory jurisdictional rule.
Tex. Fam. Code §§ 201.015, 201.317, 201.1041, 201.2041,
201.104, 201.007, 201.013; Tex. Gov’t Code §§ 54A.112, 54A.115,
54A.216, 54A.209: All confirm that the associate judge’s authority is
conditional and is extinguished by a timely de novo hearing request.
In re Laurie, 691 S.W.3d 769 (Tex. App. 2024); In re Interest of
A.L.M.-F., 593 S.W.3d 271 (Tex. 2019): The courts have repeatedly
held that once a timely request is filed, the associate judge is divested
of jurisdiction, and any further action is ultra vires.
2. Judicial Immunity and the "Complete Absence of
Jurisdiction"
Judicial immunity is a powerful doctrine, but it is not absolute. It protects
judges for acts performed in their judicial capacity, even if those acts are
erroneous, malicious, or in excess of authority. However, it does not protect
acts taken in the "complete absence of all jurisdiction."
Deyo v. Bradshaw, 14-19-00792-CV (Tex. App. Aug 05, 2021); 
Tobias v. SLP Brownwood LLC, No. 11-19-00247-CV (Tex. App.
Jun 24, 2021); West v. Robinson, 486 S.W.3d 669 (Tex. App.
2016); Smith v. Dist. Attorney Office for Wood Cnty., NO.
03-13-00220-CV (Tex. App. Oct 24, 2014); Twilligear v. Carrell,
148 S.W.3d 502 (Tex. App. 2004); Dallas County v. Halsey, 87 S.W.
3d 552 (Tex. 2002): All confirm that judicial immunity is lost when a
judge acts in the clear absence of all jurisdiction.
City of El Paso v. Heinrich, 284 S.W.3d 366 (Tex. 2009): State
officials, including judges, are not protected by sovereign immunity
when acting without legal or statutory authority.
In this case, once the respondent filed a timely request for a de novo
hearing, the associate judge had no jurisdiction to act on the emergency
motion or to compel the respondent to sign any orders related to the matters
specified in the request. Any such acts were taken in the complete absence
of jurisdiction, and judicial immunity does not apply.

The facts allege that the associate judge, after being divested of jurisdiction,

Exceptions and Caveats
While the law is clear that judicial immunity does not protect acts taken in
the complete absence of jurisdiction, courts are generally reluctant to find
that a judge acted entirely without jurisdiction unless the lack of authority is
unequivocal. Grave procedural errors or even actions in excess of authority
