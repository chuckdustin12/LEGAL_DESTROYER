potentially satisfy the elements of a federal civil RICO claim—including the
existence of an enterprise, a pattern of racketeering activity, and direct
injury to business or property—success in court will depend on the ability to
plead and prove specific, non-conclusory facts as to each defendant, and to
overcome significant obstacles such as judicial immunity for the judges
involved. Summary The RICO statute is designed to address coordinated
schemes involving multiple actors who engage in a pattern of racketeering
activity that causes direct harm to a plaintiff’s business or property. In this
case, the plaintiff alleges a broad conspiracy involving a spouse, her
associates, attorneys, and judges, with predicate acts including wire fraud,
extortion, and manipulation of judicial proceedings, all aimed at depriving
him of his home, business, and parental rights. To prevail on a federal civil
RICO claim, the plaintiff must provide detailed factual allegations showing
the existence of an enterprise, at least two related predicate acts by each
defendant, and a direct causal link between those acts and his injuries.
However, the doctrine of judicial immunity presents a formidable barrier to
RICO claims against judges for actions taken in their judicial capacity, unless
the plaintiff can show that the judges acted in the clear absence of all
jurisdiction or engaged in nonjudicial acts—standards that are rarely met in
practice. Background and Relevant Law Legislative and Regulatory
Framework The federal RICO statute, 18 U.S.C. § 1962, prohibits any person
associated with an enterprise engaged in interstate commerce from
conducting the enterprise’s affairs through a pattern of racketeering
activity. To state a civil RICO claim, a plaintiff must allege: (1) at least two
predicate racketeering offenses; (2) the existence of an enterprise affecting
interstate commerce; (3) a connection between the racketeering offenses;
and (4) an injury to business or property by reason of the above, as
explained in Civil Rico: A Tool of Advocacy (2024-01-01). The definition of
“enterprise” is broad, encompassing any group of individuals associated in
fact, whether or not a legal entity, and can include both legitimate and
illegitimate enterprises. The “pattern” requirement is satisfied by at least
two predicate acts that are related and pose a threat of continued criminal
activity, with continuity and relationship being key elements (RICO: A
Primer (2022-01-31)). Case Law The Fifth Circuit has repeatedly articulated
the essential elements of a civil RICO claim: (1) a person; (2) a pattern of
racketeering activity; and (3) a connection to the acquisition, establishment,
conduct, or control of an enterprise (Delta Truck & Tractor, Inc. v. J.I. Case
Co., 855 F.2d 241 (5th Cir. 1988); Crowe v. Henry, 43 F.3d 198 (5th Cir.
1995)). The “pattern” element requires proof of “continuity plus
relationship” among the predicate acts, and the enterprise can be any group
associated in fact (RICO: A Primer (2022-01-31)). Importantly, the Fifth
Circuit has emphasized that RICO’s reach is broad, capturing not only
insiders but also outsiders who participate directly or indirectly in the
enterprise’s affairs (U.S. v. Elliott, 571 F.2d 880 (5th Cir. 1978)). However,
courts require that a RICO plaintiff plead specific facts as to each defendant,
not mere conclusory allegations or lumping together of defendants. The
complaint must detail specific facts, dates, names, communications, or

Existence of a RICO Enterprise The plaintiff alleges the formation of an

absence of jurisdiction. The scenario described—where a judge alters a

Case Law The doctrine of judicial immunity is well established in both Texas
and federal law. Judges are generally immune from suits for damages for
acts performed in their judicial capacity and within their jurisdiction
(Stacker v. Givens-Davis, 3:23-cv-00833-D (BT) (N.D. Tex. Sep 10, 2024);
Davis v. Tarrant County, Tex., 565 F.3d 214 (5th Cir. 2009)). However, there
are two key exceptions: i. Nonjudicial (Administrative) Acts: Judicial
immunity does not apply to actions that are not taken in the judge’s judicial
capacity (Fuller v. City of Dall.; Andrews v. Murphy, 1-23-CV-0950-RP (W.D.
Tex. Nov 15, 2023); Partain v. Gabert, 13-21-00037-CV (Tex. App. Aug 26,
2021); Deyo v. Bradshaw, 14-19-00792-CV (Tex. App. Aug 05, 2021); Young v.
Jackson, CIVIL ACTION NO. H-18-1077 3 (S.D. Tex. Nov 28, 2018); Machetta
v. Millard, NO. 01-17-00857-CV (Tex. App. Oct 25, 2018); McClain v. Triana,
A-18-CV-763- RP (W.D. Tex. Sep 21, 2018); Boyd v. Salacki, A-18-CA-033-LY
(W.D. Tex. Feb 09, 2018); West v. Robinson, 486 S.W.3d 669 (Tex. App. 2016);
Malina v. Gonzales, 994 F.2d 1121 (5th Cir. 1993); Ammons v. Baldwin, 705
F.2d 1445 (5th Cir. 1983); Brewer v. Blackwell, 692 F.2d 387 (5th Cir. 1982)).
ii. Actions Taken in Complete Absence of Jurisdiction: Immunity does not
protect a judge for actions, even if judicial in nature, that are taken in the
complete absence of all jurisdiction (Enwere v. Toliver, No. 3:18-CV-1108-M-
BH (N.D. Tex. Apr 23, 2020); Feist v. Gist, NO. 09-18-00255-CV (Tex. App.
Oct 24, 2019)). To determine whether an act is judicial or administrative,
courts apply a multi-factor test, considering whether the act is one normally
performed by a judge, whether it occurred in a judicial setting, whether it
relates to a case pending before the judge, and whether it arose from an
exchange with the judge in their judicial capacity (Partain v. Gabert,
13-21-00037-CV (Tex. App. Aug 26, 2021); Deyo v. Bradshaw, 14-19-00792-
CV (Tex. App. Aug 05, 2021); Malina v. Gonzales, 994 F.2d 1121 (5th Cir.
1993); Ammons v. Baldwin, 705 F.2d 1445 (5th Cir. 1983); Brewer v.
Blackwell, 692 F.2d 387 (5th Cir. 1982)). Texas courts also apply a functional
approach, focusing on the nature of the function performed rather than the

Procedural Violations Under Texas Penal Code § 37.10, tampering with a
governmental record—including a recusal motion filed with the court—by
removing exhibits or affidavits is a criminal offense. The statute applies to
any person, including judges, and is especially serious if done with intent to
defraud or harm another (Tex. Pen. Code § 37.10). The act of altering a
recusal motion to control its outcome would likely satisfy the elements of
this offense. Procedurally, Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 18a and related
authorities require that a judge, upon receiving a recusal motion, must
either recuse themselves or forward the motion in its original form (or as a
certified copy) to the presiding judge of the administrative judicial district,
along with all related statements (In re Thompson, 330 S.W.3d 411 (Tex.
App. 2010)). Any alteration of the motion or its attachments is a clear
violation of these mandatory requirements. The Texas Supreme Court has
emphasized that a judge against whom a recusal motion is filed has only two
options: recuse themselves or refer the motion to another judge. Any further
action, such as tampering with the motion, is unauthorized and may render
subsequent orders void (Gonzalez v. Guilbot, 315 S.W.3d 533 (Tex. 2010)).
Here, these two options were not respected, and the act of modifying the
record was the option chosen by James Munford. E. The Four-Factor Test
The courts use a four-factor test to determine whether an act is judicial: (1)
whether the act is a normal judicial function; (2) whether it occurred in a
judicial setting; (3) whether it relates to a case pending before the judge;
and (4) whether it arose from an exchange with the 5 judge in their judicial
capacity (Partain v. Gabert, 13-21-00037-CV (Tex. App. Aug 26, 2021); Deyo
v. Bradshaw, 14-19-00792-CV (Tex. App. Aug 05, 2021); Malina v. Gonzales,
994 F.2d 1121 (5th Cir. 1993); Ammons v. Baldwin, 705 F.2d 1445 (5th Cir.
1983); Brewer v. Blackwell, 692 F.2d 387 (5th Cir. 1982)). Applying these
factors, the act of tampering with a recusal motion by removing exhibits and
affidavits is not a normal judicial function. It is not part of the adjudicative
process, does not occur in a judicial setting, and is not an act that arises
from an exchange with the judge in their judicial capacity. Rather, it is an
administrative or ministerial act, and thus falls outside the scope of judicial
immunity. The Texas Court of Appeals has further clarified that the
functional approach focuses on the nature of the function performed. If the
function is administrative, such as altering court records, it is not protected
by judicial immunity (Wilder v. Merritt, NO. 02-16-00477-CV (Tex. App. Nov
16, 2017)). F. Impact of Subsequent Case Law Some authorities cited, such
as Malina v. Gonzales, 994 F.2d 1121 (5th Cir. 1993), were subject to
rehearing, and Sparks v. Duval County Ranch Co., Inc., 604 F.2d 976 (5th
Cir. 1979) was superseded by Dennis v. Sparks, 449 U.S. 24 (1980).
However, the core principles regarding the exceptions to judicial immunity—
nonjudicial acts and actions taken in the absence of jurisdiction—remain
good law and are reaffirmed in more recent cases (Stacker v. Givens-Davis,
3:23-cv-00833-D (BT) (N.D. Tex. Sep 10, 2024); Davis v. Tarrant County, Tex.,
565 F.3d 214 (5th Cir. 2009). Now, we turn to analyze the judicial nature of

The federal RICO statute, 18 U.S.C. § 1962, prohibits any person associated

function; (2) whether it occurred in a judicial setting; (3) whether it relates
to a case pending before the judge; and (4) whether it arose from an
exchange with the judge in their judicial capacity. Administrative acts, such
as tampering with a recusal motion, do not meet these criteria and are not
protected by judicial immunity.
Analysis
1. Existence of a RICO Enterprise
The plaintiff alleges the formation of an enterprise involving Morgan
Michelle Myers, Daniel Kenneth Branthoover, attorneys, and court officials,
with the common purpose of depriving him of his home, business, and
parental rights. The enterprise is said to have originated with Myers and
Branthoover in Texas and Oklahoma and later expanded to include attorneys
and judges in the Texas court system.

Under federal law, an “enterprise” can be any group of individuals
associated in fact, and courts interpret this term broadly (RICO: A Primer
(2022-01-31)). The allegations describe a group acting in concert, with
coordinated actions and communications, which could satisfy the enterprise
requirement if supported by specific evidence. The Supreme Court has
rejected any requirement that the enterprise be legitimate or have an
economic motive (RICO - the rejection of an economic motive requirement.
(1995-03-22)).
2. Pattern of Racketeering Activity
A “pattern” requires at least two predicate acts that are related and pose a
threat of continued criminal activity. The plaintiff alleges multiple predicate
acts, including:
Wire fraud (e.g., interstate wire transfers, fraudulent filings via
electronic systems)
Extortion (e.g., use of judicial authority to compel compliance with
fraudulent orders)
Tampering with court records and filings
Collection of unlawful debt (e.g., child support not legally owed, based
on fraudulent documents)
Interstate travel to further the scheme (potential Travel Act violations)
These acts are alleged to be related by purpose (depriving the plaintiff of
property and business interests), participants (the same group), victims (the
plaintiff), and methods (fraudulent documents, abuse of court processes,
misuse of official positions). The continuity element is supported by the
ongoing nature of the conduct, which is said to have persisted for at least 18
months (RICO: A Primer (2022-01-31)).
To survive a motion to dismiss, the plaintiff must plead specific facts as to
each defendant, including dates, communications, and the precise role each
played in the alleged racketeering activity (Gabriel v. Outlaw, CASE NO.
3:20-CV-60-E-BK (N.D. Tex. Mar 01, 2021)). Generalized or conclusory
allegations will not suffice.

The conduct described constitutes multiple predicate acts under both Texas
and federal law:
Use of electronic filing systems and email to transmit fraudulent
documents and altered court filings (wire fraud)
Compelling compliance with fraudulent court orders, especially where
judicial authority is used to obtain property or business interests
(extortion under color of official right)
Collection of child support not legally owed, using documents with
questionable authenticity (collection of unlawful debt)
Unauthorized alteration of court filings, particularly in the context of a
recusal motion (tampering with governmental records)

4. Judicial Immunity Does Not Apply
The key defense likely to be raised by the judicial defendants is judicial
immunity. However, the law is clear that judicial immunity does not extend
to administrative or ministerial acts, nor to acts taken in the clear absence
of all jurisdiction. The alteration of a recusal motion, removal of exhibits,
and other administrative tampering with court records are not judicial acts
—they are administrative or ministerial in nature and thus fall outside the
scope of judicial immunity (Walker v. Hartman, 516 S.W.3d 71 (Tex. App.
2017); Jones v. Sherry, NO. 03-18-00279-CV (Tex. App. Jun 28, 2019)).
Moreover, Texas Penal Code § 37.10 applies to any person, including judges,
and criminalizes tampering with governmental records. The legislative
intent is clear: no one is above the law when it comes to the integrity of
governmental records, and the statute specifically contemplates criminal
liability for such conduct, regardless of the actor’s official status.
5. Liability of Non-Judicial Actors
Attorneys, private parties, and court staff do not enjoy judicial immunity for
predicate acts under RICO. The functional approach to derived judicial
immunity only protects non-judges when they perform functions intimately
associated with the judicial process and exercise discretionary judgment
comparable to that of a judge. Administrative acts such as altering court
records or participating in fraudulent filings do not meet this standard.
6. Direct Injury to Plaintiff
The plaintiff has alleged direct injury to his business and property interests,
including loss of home, business operations, and the ability to serve
interstate clients, as well as deprivation of a fair court proceeding. These
injuries are precisely the type of harm that RICO is designed to remedy
(Civil Rico: A Tool of Advocacy (2024-01-01)).
7. Court’s Remedial Powers
Federal courts are empowered to issue broad remedies to prevent, restrain,
and remedy racketeering, including injunctions, receiverships, and
constructive trusts. If the plaintiff can establish the pattern of racketeering
activity and the resulting harm, the court has ample authority to grant relief
(PAYORS, PLAYERS, AND PROXIMATE CAUSE. (2022-05-01)).
Exceptions and Caveats
While the plaintiff’s case is strong, the ultimate determination of liability will
depend on the outcome of a full investigation and judicial proceedings. The

defendants may attempt to argue that their actions were judicial in nature
or within their jurisdiction, but the weight of authority supports the
conclusion that administrative tampering with court records and
participation in a racketeering enterprise are not protected by judicial
immunity.
It is also important to note that some authorities cited in the analysis, such
as Malina v. Gonzales, 994 F.2d 1121 (5th Cir. 1993), have been subject to
rehearing or subsequent treatment. However, the core principles regarding
the exceptions to judicial immunity—nonjudicial acts and actions taken in

Conclusion
The plaintiff’s federal RICO case is well-supported by both statutory and
case law. The alleged conduct by the defendants—including judges, court
staff, attorneys, and private parties—constitutes a pattern of racketeering
activity involving predicate acts such as wire fraud, tampering with
governmental records, and extortion, none of which are protected by judicial
immunity when performed as administrative or extrajudicial acts. The
legislative framework and case law make clear that such conduct falls
outside the scope of judicial immunity and is actionable under both Texas
and federal RICO statutes. If the plaintiff can substantiate the factual
allegations with evidence and plead with the required specificity, the
defendants face significant exposure to liability, and the court has broad
authority to grant appropriate remedies.
