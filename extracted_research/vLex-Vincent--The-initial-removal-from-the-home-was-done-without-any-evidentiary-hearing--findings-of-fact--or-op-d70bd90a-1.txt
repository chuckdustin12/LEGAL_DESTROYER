Summary
The facts presented reveal a pattern of systemic due process violations and
disregard for statutory requirements in Texas family law proceedings,
particularly regarding the removal of a child from a parent without timely
hearing, findings, or opportunity to be heard. Texas Family Code provisions
and controlling Texas Supreme Court precedent require prompt adversary
hearings, diligent prosecution, and meaningful judicial review, all of which
appear to have been disregarded, resulting in prolonged deprivation of
parental and property rights without lawful justification.
Further compounding these violations are allegations of ineffective
assistance of counsel, fraudulent or coerced orders, unauthorized practice
by opposing counsel, tampering with judicial filings, and judicial actions
taken while recusal or mandamus was pending. These irregularities
undermine the legitimacy of the proceedings and may render orders void or

Background and Relevant Law
Legislative Framework
The Texas Family Code establishes strict procedural safeguards for the
removal of a child from a parent’s home. Under Tex. Fam. Code § 262.101, a
governmental entity seeking to remove a child without prior notice and
hearing must file a petition supported by a sworn affidavit demonstrating
immediate danger to the child and the necessity of removal, as well as
reasonable efforts to prevent removal. If a child is taken into possession
without a court order, Tex. Fam. Code § 262.105 requires the prompt filing
of a suit affecting the parent-child relationship, appointment of an attorney
ad litem, and an initial hearing by the next business day.
Most critically, Tex. Fam. Code § 262.201 mandates a full adversary hearing
within 14 days of removal, unless the child is returned or the temporary
order dissolved. At this hearing, the court must return the child to the
parent unless there is sufficient evidence of danger and that reasonable
efforts to prevent removal have been made. Temporary orders affecting
conservatorship or access generally require notice and a hearing, except in
narrowly defined emergencies (Tex. Fam. Code § 105.001, § 262.102).
Temporary orders changing the designation of primary residence during a
modification suit are further restricted and must be in the child’s best
interest and meet specific statutory criteria (Tex. Fam. Code § 156.006).
The Family Code also provides for dismissal of cases for want of prosecution
if the case remains dormant for over a year without trial or extension (Tex.
Fam. Code § 263.401). Parties have the right to request a de novo hearing
before the referring court following an associate judge’s order (Tex. Fam.

Case Law
The Texas Supreme Court has repeatedly emphasized the constitutional and
statutory necessity of prompt adversary hearings and findings of fact when a
child is removed from a parent. In In re E.C.R., 402 S.W.3d 239 (Tex. 2013),
the Court held that a full adversary hearing must be held within 14 days of
removal, and the child must be returned unless there is sufficient evidence
of danger and reasonable efforts to prevent removal. Similarly, In re Texas
Dept. of Family Protec. Serv., 255 S.W.3d 613 (Tex. 2008) reaffirmed these
requirements, emphasizing the necessity of timely hearings and findings.
Temporary orders excluding a parent from access or possession require
verified pleadings or affidavits and may not be rendered without notice and
hearing, except in emergencies (In re K.L.R., 162 S.W.3d 291 (Tex. 2005)).
The Court has also recognized that due process requires meaningful
appellate review and written explanations for judicial decisions, especially
where fundamental rights are at stake (In re Columbia Medical Center, 290
S.W.3d 204 (Tex. 2009)).
Due process violations and other constitutional claims must generally be
preserved in the trial court to be considered on appeal (In re S.A.P., 169 S.W.
3d 685 (Tex. 2005)), though this case has been abrogated in part by In re
Interest of C.W., NO. 02-17-00025-CV (Tex. App. May 25, 2017), which may
affect the strictness of preservation requirements.
Ineffective assistance of counsel in parental rights cases can constitute a
due process violation if counsel’s performance is so deficient as to deprive
the parent of meaningful representation, as discussed in secondary
materials.
Administrative and Local Rules
The Texas Administrative Code requires the Department of Protective and
Regulatory Services to inform parents of planned court actions, their right
to representation, and their right to attend hearings, and to provide written
notice when a child is removed without a court order (40 Tex. Admin. Code §
700.1102).
Local rules, such as those in Tarrant County, may further govern
consolidation, recusal, and prosecution diligence, but must be consistent
with state law.
